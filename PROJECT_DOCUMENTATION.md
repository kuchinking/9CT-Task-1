### My findings: 
Increases in oil prices generally corelate with an increase in inflation, both in Australia and worldwide. Higher oil prices tend to be linked with an increase in inflation, and vice versa. As fuel gets more expensive, specifically motor fuel, the cost for transportation of goods rises, increasing the price of everything and subsequently rising inflation. For example in the 1970's conflicts in the middle east halted production and export of fuel, impacting the whole world, with Australia's inflation rate seeing highs of 15+% annually during the period. Another instance of this is during covid, where oil prices decreased due to limited demand and a decrease in inflation was observed. The main issue with drawing conclusions from examples such as the 1970's fuel crisis, and COVID-19 is that other factors related to these major events can also influence inflation greatly, however an increase in inflation still generally corelates with, and can cause, an increase in inflation.

Whether oil prices cause inflation is more complicated. A major cost for consumer goods, and a player in inflation is the transportation of goods, which the cost of transportation is heavily linked with motor fuel cost. An increase in oil prices from for example a war or blockade in a major oil producing region, will cause the price of motor fuel to rise, subsequently raising the cost of transportation of goods and eventually increasing the cost of inflation. Another way an increase in oil price raise inflation is manufacturing costs. Oil is a key component in many goods, making up products such as plastics and providing energy to many manufacturing plants. An increase in even the price for packaging goods, from an oil shock, would have an effect on inflation. From transportation, to manufacturing, to packaging, oil prices definitively cause an impact on inflation, with the Reserve Bank of Australia, the governments bank saying "In general energy price increases add to inflation for a period."
